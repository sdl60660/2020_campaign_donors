One thing that becomes clear is how much larger presidential fundraising operations are relative to even the most prolific congressional fundraisers. Another is the size, in particular, of the Bloomberg and Steyer campaigns.

By separating out self-contributions, we can better understand and compare the campaigns' fundraising operations and it now becomes clear that Bloomberg and Steyer's massive fundraising advantages are due almost entirely to the money that they gave their own campaigns.

We can take this a step further, by separating out transfers (and other small, miscellaneous fundraising sources) from individual contributions, at the top. Note how much of Trump's fundraising is made up of transfers from other committees. These transfers mostly come from joint fundraising committees such as the "Make America Great Again Committee", which splits its money between Trump's campaign and the RNC.

Individual contributions can then be split between larger donors ($200 or more) on the left and smaller donors (less than $200) on the right.


Note, for example, that smaller donors make up nearly 80% of Rep. Ocasio-Cortez's campaign fundraising, while making up just a small fraction of the fundraising for Tom Steyer's presidential campaign (despite him successfully meeting the 130,000 donor requirement for the Democratic debates).

Finally, we can introduce money going to Super PACs/Carey Committees. These committees do not have formal ties to any parties or campaigns, but many of them frequently spend on behalf of candidates and can often be broadly categorized as liberal (blue) or conservative (red). They can accept unlimited contributions from individuals, while individual contributions to a campaign's primary committee cannot exceed $2,800.


In total, these committees have raised about $1.2 billion in the 2020 cycle, a very large sum of money, but just 12% of the nearly $10 billion raised by campaigns (and notably just a few million more than Bloomberg's contributions to his own campaign).

While Super PAC money isn't formally tied to campaigns, there are several PACs which act solely in support of a individual candidates (with varying levels of coordination). We can map this money to the major presidential candidates above to get a clearer picture of where all of their financial support comes from, direct or indirect.


Despite large sums of money going to presidential candidates, the vast majority of Super PAC money is not affiliated with individual candidates, but rather in broad support of congressional majorities or liberal/conservative causes. The two largest Super PACS support Democratic and Republican senate majorities, respectively.

We can learn a lot about the donors supporting various campaigns by using demographic data from their zipcodes.


Here, candidates have been plotted according to the percentage of their donors who live in majority-white zipcodes, on the x-axis, and the percentage of their donors who live in zipcodes with high bachelors' degree attainment (top 25%), on the y-axis.


Candidates with more than 15,000 known donors have been included. The size of a candidate's bubble represents the number of known donors.

Note that donors to the Republican campaigns tend to come more frequently from majority-white zipcodes, and less frequently from zipcodes with high bachelors degree attainment.

For Democratic candidates, it is the opposite. Their donors are often from more racially diverse zipcodes, and zipcodes with high bachelors degree attainment. However, there is a significant spread among Democratic candidates.

Look, for example, at the gap between donors to Bernie Sanders' campaign on the left, Amy Klobuchar's campaign to the right, indicating that Klobuchar's campaign drew many more donors from majority-white zipcodes.

Here the y-axis has been switched to indicate the percentage of donors who come from high-income zipcodes (top 25%). These zipcodes produce a very large percentage of campaign contributions overall (note that the y-axis is clipped at the bottom), but there remains a significant gap between parties and between candidates.


While a little less stark, the divide between parties holds if we look at income level instead of bachelors attainment. The divide between different Democratic party candidates, however, is much wider when looking at income than it was for bachelors attainment.

Notes

Contribution data runs through 6/30/2020.

Unique donors are determined using a combination of first name, last name and zipcode. This creates the possibility of both false positives (two Jane Smiths in the same zipcode) and false negatives (Jonathan Smith sometimes donates as Jon Smith, or Jon Smith moves homes during the election cycle). Others who have worked with donor data sometimes require a matching employer name as well. This creates a very small chance of a false positive (it's very unlikely that there are two Jane Smiths living in the same zipcode with the same employer), but creates many more false negatives (Jon Smith might change jobs mid-election cycle or he might mark his employer as "Smith High School" on one donation and "Smith H.S." on another). On balance, when looking at donor overlap, I decided that requiring a matching employer was too stringent of a requirement and would lead to missing too many donor overlaps. As it is, the analysis very likely underrepresents the amount of overlap due to false negatives, but it misses them uniformly, across candidates.

The use of the WinRed fundraising platform among Republican candidates is relatively new and still less prevalent than the ActBlue platform among Democratic candidates. Since much of the data regarding smaller-money donors (<$200) comes from the platforms' filings, GOP candidates generally have fewer identified donors than Democratic candidates. Even among candidates who have adopted these platforms, many small transactions take place outside and the campaigns are only required to report contributions from donors who give more at least $200 in aggregate. This means we don't have a true donor count for any campaign, but we can generally get much closer for Democratic ones.

Presidential campaigns with $500,000+ in Super PAC money that is represented, but not labeled, include Amy Klobuchar, Jay Inslee, Kamala Harris, Corey Booker, Andrew Yang, and John Hickenlooper.

The support of the Vote Nurses Values PAC, the Super PAC affiliated with the country's largest nurses union, for the Sanders campaign is somewhat controversial.
