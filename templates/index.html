This map plots all money received by 2020 campaign committees based on the home state of the donor. Each dot represents $1 million contributed and they are colored by the party of the candidates (with yellow representing Libertarians and gray representing Independents). Hover over any state to see totals by office.

Note the two large islands off of the east coast. The first represents money that candidates gave to their own campaigns. More than 85% of this money came from two candidates, Michael Bloomberg and Tom Steyer. This money can be tied to a donor location, but has been removed from the map for clarity.


Just north of Puerto Rico sits all money that cannot be tied to a 2020 donor. This includes any donations totaling less than $200 and not contributed through the popular ActBlue/WinRed platforms. It also includes money transfered to the campaign from other committees, most notably prior campaign committees for the candidates (e.g. Trump 2016 money rolled into the Trump 2020 campaign).

We can split the money by party and see that much more money has gone to Democrats than Republicans.

But by if we divide by office type, it becomes clear that that gap is mostly due to the presidential race, where Democratic donors contributed significant sums of money to over two dozen campaigns.

Here, we can see the top fundraisers across office type. Note that the totals for all remaining candidates have been moved to the right side.


One thing that becomes clear is the size of presidential fundraising operations relative to even the most prolific congressional fundraisers. Another is the magnitude of (primarily self-funded) Bloomberg and Steyer campaigns.

By separating out self-contributions, we can better understand and compare the campaigns' fundraising operations.

We can take this a step further, by separating out transfers (and "other" fundraising sources) from individual contributions, at the top. Note the legend in the top right, for clarity.

Finally, individual contributions can be split between large-dollar ($200 or more) donors on the left and small-dollar (less than $200) donors on the right.


Note, for example, that small-dollar donors make up nearly 80% of Congresswoman Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez's campaign fundraising, while making up just a tiny fraction of the fundraising for Tom Steyer's presidential campaign (despite him successfully meeting the 130,000 donor requirement for the Democratic debates).

We can learn a lot about the donors powering campaigns with just their zipcodes.


Here, candidates have been plotted according to the percentage of their donors who live in majority-white zipcode, on the x-axis, and the percentage of their donors who live in zipcodes with high bachelors degree attainment (top 25%), on the y-axis. Candidates with more than 15,000 known donors have been included.

Note that donors to the Republican campaigns tend to come more frequently from majority-white zipcodes, but less frequently from zipcodes with high bachelors attainment.

For Democratic candidates, it is the opposite. Their donors are often from more racially diverse zipcodes, and zipcodes with high bachelors attainment. However, there is a significant spread among Democratic candidates, creating sometimes interesting clusters.

Look, for example, at the gap between donors to Bernie Sanders' campaign on the left, Amy Klobuchar's campaign, and congressional candidate Elissa Slotkin's campaign in the top-right corner.

Note that this trend holds if we look at income level instead of bachelors attainment. Here the y-axis has been switched to indicate the percentage of donors who come from high-income zipcodes (top 25%). These zipcodes produce most campaign contributions overall (note that the y-axis is clipped at the bottom), but there remains a significant gap between parties and between candidates.

Notes

Contribution data runs through 6/30/2020. Unique donors are determined using a combination of first name, last name and zipcode. This creates the possibility of both false positives (two Jane Smiths in the same zipcode) and false negatives (Jonathan Smith sometimes donates as Jon Smith, or Jon Smith moves homes during the election cycle). Others who have worked with donor data sometimes require a matching employer name as well. This creates a very small chance of a false positive (it's very unlikely that there are two Jane Smiths living in the same zipcode with the same employer), but creates many more false negatives (Jon Smith might change jobs mid-election cycle or he might mark his employer as "Smith High School" on one donation and "Smith H.S." on another). On balance, when looking at donor overlap, I decided that requiring a matching employer was too stringent of a requirement and would lead to missing too many donor overlaps. As it is, the analysis very likely underrepresents the amount of overlap due to false negatives, but it misses them uniformly, across candidates.
